Modern web applications are only as good as their accessibility. In this episode of the Howest IT Student Podcast, I speak with Jara Puype, an Angular developer and founder of Digidee, a company offering digital support services. The conversation focused on the reality of digital exclusion and how developers can build more accessible systems.

---

## The Origin of Digidee

After completing her studies, Jara noticed that many citizens struggled with everyday digital platforms. This prompted her to establish Digidee, providing computer courses and assistance for tasks like system updates, setting up government services like ItsMe, and giving advice in phishing cases.

## Practical Usability Friction

Jara highlighted several real-world examples where software design decisions created barriers:

### ItsMe
A recent update on Belgium's popular identity platform ItsMe removed phone number verification, requiring QR scanning instead. For users who had previously denied camera permissions, the application failed to guide them on how to adjust their system settings, causing widespread lockouts.

### AI-Enhanced Phishing
The styling and layout of phishing communications have become highly accurate, removing standard indicators like spelling errors. Jara recounted receiving a highly polished message requesting an account fee. Non-technical users often rely solely on logos rather than checking the email address of the sender.

---

## Actionable Developer Guidelines

The interview concluded with concrete suggestions for frontend engineers:

- **Demographic Testing**: Ensure application testing includes users outside the core development team, particularly older demographics, to identify friction in navigation and layout vocabulary.
- **Helpful Error Message**: Make sure error messages are non-technical, and most importantly explain how the error state can be resolved.
- **Simplifying Access**: Use standard email addresses or phone numbers as identifiers rather than custom usernames, and maintain a consistent authentication flow.
- **Physical Security Realities**: While password managers are recommended, a physical notebook kept securely at home remains a practical and secure solution for users who struggle with complex digital workflows.

---

## Summary

Designing accessible software is not just about meeting compliance checkers; it is about empathy for the end user. Ensuring clear failure states, descriptive navigation, and consistent patterns helps close the digital divide. Many thanks to Jara Puype for sharing her experiences from the field.
