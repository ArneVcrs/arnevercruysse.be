Science Day (Dag van de Wetenschap) brought a lively crowd of curious minds to Howest's MaM in Brugge. I spent the day volunteering at one of the exhibits, helping families explore the mathematics of chance.

---

## Exploring the Mind- and Makerspace (MaM)

The event took place at Howest’s **Mind- and Makerspace (MaM)**, an open-access lab situated near the Brugge station. Usually, this space is a playground for makers and students, filled with 3D printers, laser cutters, ceramics ovens, and audio-visual recording studios. Guided by the values of learning, creating, and sharing, the MaM was the perfect setting to host interactive public science demonstrations.

Aside from our Mathsino booth, the MaM hosted a variety of other engaging science exhibits. Visitors could peer through high-resolution microscopes to inspect their own skin cells or get hands-on experience programming Micro:bit microcontrollers to display custom messages and control small motors.

## Introducing the "Mathsino"

Our booth, the **Mathsino**, was set up as a mock casino. The goal was to teach children and their parents that behind every game of chance lies predictable mathematics. By using casino-style setups, we made abstract statistical concepts tangible.

---

## The House Edge in Roulette

One of our main displays focused on the classic roulette wheel. Visitors placed play-money bets while we analyzed the specific mathematics that guarantees the casino's profitability:

*   **The Illusion of Fair Play**: Players often assume that betting on even-money options like red or black yields a 50/50 chance of winning.
*   **The Pocket Breakdown**: A standard European roulette wheel has 37 pockets in total: numbers 1 through 36 (18 red, 18 black) plus a single green zero (0).
*   **The Probability Math**: If you bet on red, you have 18 winning pockets and 19 losing pockets (the 18 black pockets plus the 1 green zero pocket). This means your probability of winning is 18/37 (approximately 48.65%), while your probability of losing is 19/37 (approximately 51.35%).
*   **Expected Value**: The expected value (EV) for a 1 play-money bet on red or black is calculated as:
    
    EV = (18/37 * +1) + (19/37 * -1) = -1/37 (approximately -2.70%)

    This negative expected value of -2.70% means that for every 100 play-money units wagered, a player can expect to lose an average of 2.70 units. This exact 2.70% margin is the house edge. Over hundreds of spins, the Law of Large Numbers guarantees the house will steadily retain this percentage of all wagers.

## Rolling Two Dice: The Gaussian Curve

Another popular demonstration involved rolling a pair of dice. We invited kids and parents to roll pairs of dice, calculate the sum, and place a cotton ball in the column corresponding to that sum on our large vertical tracker grid:

*   **Generating the Bell Curve**: As the day progressed and hundreds of cotton balls accumulated, a clear bell-shaped distribution (the Gauss curve) emerged inside the tracker columns.
*   **The Statistics of 7**: We explained why 7 is the most frequent outcome, possessing six distinct combinations (1+6, 2+5, 3+4, etc.), while 2 and 12 are rare anomalies with only a single combination each.

Seeing parents and children realize how individual random rolls aggregate into a predictable Gauss curve was incredibly rewarding.
